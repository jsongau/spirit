# The Palace Triangle (三方四正) — teach-back research

## Concept in one paragraph

In Purple Star Astrology (Zi Wei Dou Shu), no single palace is read on its own. Every palace is read as part of a four-cell group called 三方四正 (Sān Fāng Sì Zhèng), which teachers usually translate as "three directions and four alignments" or "the triangle and the four squares." The group is made of the palace you care about, the two palaces that sit at the trine points 120 degrees away, and the palace directly across the chart at 180 degrees, called the opposite palace (對宮). Read together, these four cells turn a flat, one-line reading into a rounded picture, so a topic like career is judged from the whole neighbourhood rather than from one box. Classical readings weight these connections in a set order: stars sitting in the palace itself carry the most direct force, the opposite palace comes next, and the two trine palaces come third. Different schools describe the feel of this differently, but the frame itself is one of the first things almost every teacher establishes.

## How good teachers explain it (the mechanism, why no room is read alone, why the opposite palace outweighs the two trines)

The clearest teachers open with an analogy rather than a diagram. Flourish Astrology frames the group as a company board. The palace you are studying is the CEO, and before any real decision is made the CEO consults three senior people, the finance side (Wealth), the operations side (Career), and the outward-facing general manager (Travel). The point of the analogy is that a person's outcome in any area is a committee result, not a solo call, so reading one palace alone is like quoting the CEO while ignoring the rest of the meeting. The sng-mia academy makes the same case more plainly: read only the Career Palace and you see work attitude, add the Life Palace and you see temperament, add Wealth and you see earning power, add Travel and you see outside opportunity. The four together give a three-dimensional slice of a life area, which is why teachers insist the reader "reads all four positions and neglects none."

On why the opposite palace outweighs the two trines, teachers give a structural reason and a relational one. Structurally, the opposite palace sits directly across at 180 degrees and points straight back at the target, so its stars act on the target head-on, while the trine palaces meet it at an angle and feed in more like supporting resources. Several schools put rough weights on this to help beginners: the palace itself is full strength, the opposite palace is close behind it, and the trines sit a notch lower. Relationally, teachers describe the opposite palace as the complement or mirror of the target, a yin-yang pair that completes one dimension of life. Life pairs with Travel as inner self and outer self, Wealth pairs with Spirit (福德) as earning and keeping, Spouse pairs with Career as private life and public role. A common teaching formula calls the target palace the "essence" (體, tǐ) and the opposite palace the "use" or "function" (用, yòng): one is what a thing is, the other is how it plays out in the world, and you need both to see the whole. The strongest proof teachers point to is the empty-palace rule. When a palace holds none of the fourteen major stars, the reader borrows the major stars from the opposite palace to interpret it, usually treating the borrowed force as weaker (many teachers estimate roughly 60 to 70 percent). No one borrows from the trine palaces this way, and that asymmetry is the everyday evidence that the opposite palace carries more weight than the two trines. The trines, by contrast, are taught as the resource line: when the trine palaces hold good stars, they "shine into" the target and strengthen it, and difficult stars or transformations in a trine can quietly drag the target down.

## Common student errors (3-5, each one sentence)

- Reading a single palace in isolation and calling a verdict, when classical method requires reading the whole four-cell group first.
- Assuming an opposite palace with harsh stars automatically damages the target, when teachers say that external energy can act as pressure or as motivation depending on the rest of the chart.
- Ignoring the two trine palaces as if they were minor, when they are the resource line that supports or weakens the target and can carry transformations into it.
- Treating "stars sitting in the same palace" and "stars meeting from across the group" as equal in force, when the taught order is same palace, then opposite, then trine.
- Trying to name a specific outcome such as an exact job, illness, or event from one palace alone, when even a full triangle read is only associated with tendencies and needs the wider chart and timing.

## Model teach-back answer — beginner register (2-4 sentences a newcomer could say)

In Purple Star Astrology you never read one palace by itself. You read it together with three partners: the palace directly across the chart, called the opposite palace, and the two palaces that form a triangle with it. The opposite palace acts like a mirror and pulls on your palace the hardest after the palace's own stars, and the two triangle palaces add supporting energy. Reading all four together is what turns a thin guess into a rounded picture.

## Model teach-back answer — practitioner register (2-4 sentences with the nuance)

The 三方四正 frame reads any palace alongside its opposite palace at 180 degrees and its two trine palaces at 120 degrees, with the classical weighting running same palace, then opposite, then trine. The opposite palace outranks the trines because it sits head-on and works as the complement of the target, the "function" to the target's "essence," which is also why an empty palace borrows its major stars from the opposite palace and not from the trines, at a discounted strength. The trine palaces are read as the resource and support line that shines into the target and can lift it or, through an unfavourable transformation, quietly pull it down. In practice you set the tone from this four-cell group first, then layer in the flanking neighbours, brightness, and the timing charts before you commit to any reading.

## Responsible phrasing notes (how to keep it non-deterministic)

- Frame every claim as tradition or method, not fact: say "classical readings associate," "this school teaches," or "the frame tends to show," rather than "this will happen."
- Keep the language about tendencies and emphasis, for example "leans toward" or "is often read as," and avoid fixed verdicts about health, death, wealth, marriage, or disaster.
- Name that schools differ. The weighting order is widely shared, but the exact strength given to the opposite palace and the borrowing percentage vary by teacher, so present them as common practice rather than settled law.
- Point out that the triangle is a starting frame, not a full reading. A responsible teach-back adds that brightness, transformations, flanking palaces, and the ten-year and annual charts all still have to be weighed.
- Present the chart as a tool for reflection and choice. Several of these teaching sources stress that the natal picture sets a pattern and that timing and personal effort shape how it actually plays out.
