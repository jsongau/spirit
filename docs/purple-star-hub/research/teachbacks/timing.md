# Bureau and Timing (五行局, 大限, 流年) — teach-back research

## Concept in one paragraph (the natal chart is terrain, timing is the overlay)

In Purple Star Astrology (紫微斗數) the birth chart is a fixed map of terrain. It sets out twelve palaces, the stars that sit in each, and the brightness those stars carry, and this layout does not change across a life. What changes is which part of the map you are standing on. Classical teaching separates 命 (the natal blueprint, the innate potential) from 運 (the moving fortune that passes over it). The Five Element Bureau (五行局) is the clock that decides when the moving layers begin and how the decades are counted. The Decade Doors (大限, ten-year major limits) light up one palace at a time, and the Year Wave (流年, the annual layer) passes over the whole grid once a year. A common image in teaching is that the natal chart is the land you build on, the decade is the room of the house you are living in, and the year is the weather that arrives at the door. Nothing in timing rewrites the terrain. It only decides which promises in the terrain get their turn to surface.

## How good teachers explain the Bureau and the decade clock

Good teachers introduce the Bureau (局) as the setting that governs how the whole chart unfolds rather than as a fortune in itself. It is derived from the birth year Heavenly Stem (天干) and the Earthly Branch of the Life Palace (命宮地支), read through the 納音 (Nayin) system and a classical lookup, so two people born the same day at different hours can land in different Bureaus because the Life Palace shifts with the birth hour. There are five, each tied to an element and a starting age: 水二局 (Water 2), 木三局 (Wood 3), 金四局 (Metal 4), 土五局 (Earth 5), 火六局 (Fire 6). The number does two jobs. It helps position 紫微 and the main stars, and it sets the age at which the first decade begins, so the Bureau number is roughly the age the decade clock starts ticking. A Water 2 chart begins its first decade around age 2 and cycles quickly, while a Fire 6 chart starts later around age 6 and gives each decade more time. Teachers stress that this is timing, not content: the Bureau tells you when each decade opens, never what happens inside it. As one teaching source puts it, the Bureau is the clock and the stars are the story. The decade clock itself then advances one palace every ten years, and the direction of counting (forward or backward around the grid) depends on the Yin or Yang polarity of the birth year together with sex, which is why two charts can move around the same twelve palaces in opposite order.

## Decade Doors vs the Year Wave (the door sets the decade's theme, the year knocks on one room)

The Decade Door (大限) and the Year Wave (流年) are different scales of the same idea, and mixing them up is the classic beginner mistake. The Decade Door activates one palace as the "life battlefield" for roughly ten years, so its stars, brightness, and the decade's own Four Transformations (四化) set the broad theme of that stretch: which domain of life is under the light, where pressure and opportunity tend to concentrate. The Year Wave is the annual layer laid on top, and it moves fast, touching a different configuration each year. Teachers describe reading these through 疊宮 (palace layering or stacking): you place the natal chart, then the decade chart, then the annual chart over the same grid and watch where a moving palace lands on a natal one. That overlap is what focuses attention. A useful teaching contrast is that the decade sets the decade's theme while the year knocks on one room within it. Duration also grades intensity. Because a decade lasts ten years, its themes tend to build slowly and matter more; the same configuration seen only at the annual level touches roughly one year and reads lighter, and at the monthly or daily level lighter still. Some schools add 小限 as a separate annual personal layer and distinguish it from 流年, teaching that 流年 leans toward outer circumstance you do not control while 小限 leans toward your own state and response. Different schools weight these layers differently, so a responsible teacher names which layer they are reading before drawing any conclusion.

## Natal promise vs temporal trigger, and responsible forecasting

The organizing principle is that a natal chart holds promises and the moving layers decide their timing. Classical teaching frames this as 命 versus 運: the birth chart shows what a life can hold, and the decade and year cycles decide when and how strongly that shows up. A frequently taught image is the seed and the season, where the terrain holds the seed and the timing is the season that either helps it grow or keeps it dormant. Two consequences follow. First, timing cannot manufacture what the terrain never held, and it cannot erase what the terrain clearly holds; it changes when a theme surfaces and how it feels, not the underlying blueprint. Second, a theme usually reads strongest when several layers point at the same palace at once, which teachers call an activation or resonance point. Responsible forecasting stays inside this logic. It speaks about a domain being under pressure or well supported for a period and about the wisest posture to take toward it, rather than naming fixed outcomes. The whole tradition, in its own self-description, is not fatalistic: it treats the chart as a way to see the forces at work and to choose timing and effort wisely, which is why teachers pair "know your terrain" with "know your season" and leave the choice with the person. This means timing language should read as "this area is highlighted this year, engage it consciously," never as a dated verdict.

## Common student errors (3-5, each one sentence)

- Reading the Bureau number as a fortune or a personality grade, when it only sets the decade start age and the star-placement rhythm.
- Collapsing the decade and the year into one voice, so a slow ten-year theme gets misread as a single dramatic year (or the reverse).
- Forgetting 疊宮, and judging a moving palace on its own instead of seeing where it lands on the natal terrain underneath.
- Treating a natal promise as a scheduled event, when the terrain only says what is possible and the moving layers decide whether and when it surfaces.
- Escalating a heavy-sounding configuration into a fixed outcome and a date, instead of naming it as a period of pressure to engage with awareness.

## Model teach-back answer — beginner register (2-4 sentences)

Think of your birth chart as fixed terrain: twelve rooms with fixed contents that do not change. The Five Element Bureau (五行局) is the clock that sets when your ten-year decades begin, the Decade Door (大限) lights up one room at a time to set the theme of that stretch, and the Year Wave (流年) is the weather that passes over the whole map each year. Classical readings associate the decade with the big theme and the year with which room gets knocked on within it. None of the timing changes your terrain; it just decides which part gets its turn and asks you to engage that area consciously.

## Model teach-back answer — practitioner register (2-4 sentences)

Separate 命 from 運 before you say anything: the natal 十二宮, star brightness, and natal 四化 hold the promise, while the Bureau sets the decade cadence and counting direction from the year's Yin or Yang polarity and sex. Read timing by 疊宮, stacking the 大限 and 流年 palaces over the natal grid and noting where moving 四化 fall onto natal palaces, and grade intensity by duration so a decade-length theme carries more weight than the same pattern seen only for a year. Where several layers converge on one palace, name it as a live activation of that domain, and keep 小限 and 流年 distinct if your school reads both. Frame the output as "this domain is under pressure or well supported this cycle, here is the posture that fits," and never as a dated event.

## Responsible phrasing notes

- Say "this domain is highlighted / under pressure / well supported this cycle" and "engage it consciously," not a fixed outcome.
- Use hedged, teaching language: "classical readings associate," "tends to," "different schools teach," "this configuration is often read as."
- Never state a date of death, divorce, windfall, illness, or disaster, and never imply the chart schedules such an event.
- Keep the natal-versus-timing frame explicit, since it protects the person's agency: terrain shows what is possible, season shows when to lean in or rest.
- Name which layer you are reading (natal, decade, year, or personal annual) so no slow theme gets inflated into a single dramatic year.
- Present challenging cycles as periods for consolidation, care, and awareness, framed as preparation rather than punishment.
