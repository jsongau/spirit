# The Four Transformations (四化) — teach-back research

## Concept in one paragraph (forces attach to stars, keyed to a Heavenly Stem)

The Four Transformations (四化 Sì Huà) are not stars in their own right. They are four forces that latch onto whichever Main Stars a chart happens to hold, and the choice of which star each force lands on is keyed to a Heavenly Stem (天干). In the natal chart that stem comes from the birth year (and in some schools the birth day), and the same mechanism re-fires for each ten-year and annual luck period using that period's stem. A common teaching image is a tree through the seasons: the tree stays the same, but its face changes with the time of year. In the same way a star keeps its core nature while a transformation colours how it behaves. The four forces are 化祿 (Huà Lù, flow or gain), 化權 (Huà Quán, power or control), 化科 (Huà Kē, recognition or exposure), and 化忌 (Huà Jì, the void or hook). Good teachers stress from the first lesson that none of the four is simply good or bad; they describe how energy moves through a life, and each has an upside and a cost depending on the star it rides and the palace it sits in.

## How good teachers explain each force

**化祿 Huà Lù — flow.** The character 祿 originally meant an official's salary, a steady and lawful income with standing attached, so teachers generalise it to smooth circulation of any good thing: money coming in, an opening appearing, a relationship warming, work moving without friction. When 祿 activates a star, that star's area of life receives an easier supply of energy. The caution teachers add is that the gain always takes the shape of the star it rides, so it is a specific kind of abundance rather than a blank uplift, and "more" is not automatically "better."

**化權 Huà Quán — power.** The character 權 points to authority and the right to decide. Where 祿 opens the flow, 權 is the hand that shapes it: leadership over a domain, decisive influence, the drive to take charge. Teachers often pair the two and note that flow without power tends to describe someone who attracts luck but ends up directing it for others, while power without flow describes authority that outruns its reward. The honest caveat is that 權 amplifies whatever the star already leans toward, so on an already forceful star it can tip into rigidity, control, or a habit of pushing too hard.

**化科 Huà Kē — recognition.** The character 科 carries the sense of being seen, named, and given standing. Teachers describe it as a spotlight: reputation, exposure, being spoken well of, help from mentors or well-placed people. It tends to be the gentlest of the four and often reads as a smoothing, face-saving influence. The nuance good teachers add is that a spotlight only shows what is there; recognition can be surface rather than substance, and 科 is not automatically a blessing since exposure can also mean simply being noticed at an awkward moment.

**化忌 Huà Jì — the Hook.** The character 忌 clusters around taboo, avoidance, and attachment, and beginners are quick to read it as "the bad one." Better teachers reframe it as a felt lack, a place where the person never quite feels full. A helpful image is hunger: an empty stomach is not a curse, it is what makes you get up and find food. So 忌 marks the domain a person keeps returning to, works hardest at, and cannot easily let go of. It tends to bring friction and a sense that the result never fully justifies the effort, which is exactly why it also tends to be where a person develops unusual depth over time.

## Why the natal 化忌 is the most weighted, framed responsibly

Across teaching sources the birth-year 化忌 (生年化忌) is treated as the single most information-dense mark in the chart, often called something like the chart's baseline reference line. The reason is that it names the one domain a person is most attached to and most easily feels short in, and because our attachments quietly drive our choices, that lack tends to organise a great deal of behaviour around it. Teachers are careful to frame this as a recurring pressure point and a place of growth, not a doom sentence. A worked example many use: someone with a wealth-star 化忌 in the wealth palace often feels short of money even when they are objectively well off, and that very feeling of "not enough" is frequently what pushes them to build wealth in the first place. So the natal 化忌 reads as a growth edge, the area where easy short-cuts are not available and therefore the area where the person can go deepest. It gains further weight through the pairing "祿隨忌走" (flow follows the hook): the place a person feels emptiest is often the place they pour their gains back into, which is why teachers read 化祿 and 化忌 together rather than in isolation. Responsible teaching keeps this at the level of tendency and motivation and does not turn it into a fixed verdict about health, wealth, marriage, or misfortune.

## Common student errors (3-5, each one sentence)

- Treating 化忌 as a curse or a guaranteed disaster instead of a felt lack that tends to drive effort and growth.
- Treating 化祿 as automatically good and reading "more" as "better," when the gain only ever takes the specific shape of the star it rides.
- Reading a single transformation in isolation instead of together with the star it lands on, the palace it sits in, and the other three forces.
- Confusing an inner sense of lack with an outer fact, for example reading a wealth-palace 化忌 as literal poverty rather than a subjective feeling of never having enough.
- Forgetting that the four re-fire for each luck period, so a birth-chart transformation and a yearly transformation on the same star describe different layers and can even point opposite ways.

## Model teach-back answer — beginner register (2-4 sentences)

The Four Transformations are four forces that attach to your existing stars, and which star each one lands on is set by a Heavenly Stem from your birth date. Think of them as flow (祿), power (權), recognition (科), and the hook (忌). The hook, 化忌, is not a curse; it is more like hunger, the spot where you never quite feel full and so keep working at it. None of the four is simply good or bad, they just show how energy tends to move through different parts of your life.

## Model teach-back answer — practitioner register (2-4 sentences)

The 四化 key off the relevant Heavenly Stem and modify Main Stars rather than standing alone, so I always read a transformation together with its star, its palace, and its three siblings. I weight the natal 生年化忌 most heavily because it names the chart-holder's core attachment and growth edge, and I read it alongside 化祿 through the "祿隨忌走" pairing to see where their gains flow back in. I frame 化忌 as a recurring pressure point that tends to build depth, not as a fixed misfortune, and I keep the register at the level of tendency, checking the activated star's nature before saying anything about intensity. Then I layer the ten-year and annual stems on top, since the same four forces re-fire each period and supply the timing overlay that makes the chart move.

## Responsible phrasing notes

- Lead with "classical readings associate," "tends to," and "different schools teach," and keep every claim at the level of tendency and motivation rather than fixed outcome.
- Never turn a transformation, and especially 化忌, into a deterministic statement about health, death, wealth, marriage, or disaster; frame the Hook as a recurring pressure point and a place of growth.
- Say plainly that none of the four is simply good or bad, so a student does not celebrate 化祿 or panic at 化忌 on sight.
- Distinguish an inner felt lack from an outer fact, and distinguish the natal layer from the luck-period layers, so a reading is not collapsed into a single verdict.
- Use traditional characters and plain English, and avoid hype or dramatic language when describing difficult placements.
